Unit 5: Interviews, Survey Methods, and Questionnaire Design

← Previous Unit Next Unit →

Unit Overview

Unit 5 focused on the use of interviews, surveys, and questionnaire design in qualitative and quantitative research. Then we explored pre and post testing methods.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the role of interviews and surveys in research and determine their relevance to your study.
  • Differentiate between surveys and questionnaires and understand their unique purposes.
  • Learn how to design effective questionnaires to collect qualitative, quantitative, or mixed-method data.
  • Explore pre- and post-testing methods and their application in research.
  • Analyse data collected through interviews, surveys, and questionnaires effectively.

This section includes the artefacts completed during this unit.

Inappropriate Use of Surveys Case Study Reflective Activity

The Cambridge Analytica scandal (Confessore, 2018) is a high-profile example of how surveys can be manipulated to gather personal data for alternative purposes. Cambridge Analytica accessed data from approximately 87 million Facebook users through a personality quiz even though only 270 thousand people took the survey as they also collected data from the participants' Facebook friends, they then used this data to build psychological profiles for targeted political ads for the 2016 US presidential election.

Other high-profile examples of companies who have used surveys inappropriately are Target who in 2012 used survey and purchase data to predict customers' life events so they could target ads to individuals (Duhigg, 2012) and TikTok who in 2020 used surveys intended to improve UX to collect data about their users for targeted advertising and financial gain (Newton, 2020).

These types of exploitative surveys raise plenty of legal, social and ethical issues — for example, these companies could be found to be in violation of human rights laws related to informed consent as the victims weren’t fully aware of how their data would be used, and they could be found to be in breach of privacy laws as at a result they could receive a significant fine and suffer significant reputation damage.

Questionnaires Wiki Activity

Introduction

For this activity, I will analyse and critique the Census 2021 Household Questionnaire from the Office for National Statistics (ONS, 2021). The six specific questions I will focus on are:

  1. What is your date of birth?
  2. What is your sex?
  3. How would you describe your ethnic group?
  4. Do you have any long-term health conditions or disabilities?
  5. What is your current employment status?
  6. How many people live in your household, and what is their relationship to you?

Format and Structure Analysis

Overall, the questionnaire is clear and straightforward, using categorised sections to group similar questions effectively. However, its length may overwhelm some respondents, potentially affecting completion rates.

Question Analysis

While collecting respondents' dates of birth, provides valuable demographic data, this question may cause discomfort for individuals who prefer not to share their age. Additionally, the variation in date formats across countries could confuse respondents whose primary nationality is not English. To address these issues, I recommend requesting respondents' age range instead. This would provide the necessary data without causing confusion or discomfort.

Although asking about sex is a reasonable question, the limited response options may exclude or offend individuals in the LGBTQIA+ community. A lack of inclusivity can lead to feelings of marginalisation. To avoid this, I suggest expanding the response options to include categories such as "Non-binary," "Prefer not to say", or an open-text option to reflect modern societal diversity.

The question on ethnicity is important for collecting data on diversity. However, the response categories are often broad and may not adequately represent all ethnic groups. This lack of specificity could lead to confusion or frustration among respondents unsure which category applies to them. Adding more granular options or an open-text field for "Other" would enhance inclusivity and accuracy.

The question on long-term health conditions and disabilities is limited to "Yes" or "No" responses, as such it is overly simplistic. It misses the opportunity to gather detailed data about the nature of respondents' conditions or disabilities. A follow-up question allowing respondents to specify the type of condition would provide more meaningful insights.

Whilst understanding employment status is essential for analysing labour market trends, the limited response options exclude less common forms of work, such as gig economy roles or unpaid caregiving. This question could be improved by offering a broader range of employment categories or an open-text option.

The question about household composition requires respondents to categorise relationships into predefined roles, which may not reflect modern family dynamics. Adding an "Other" option or allowing respondents to specify relationships would accommodate diverse household arrangements.

Conclusion

While the Census questionnaire is a valuable tool for collecting demographic and household data in England and Wales, several questions lack inclusivity and flexibility. Expanding response options to reflect modern societal diversity, including a section for participant feedback, and incorporating follow-up questions where necessary would enhance the quality and completeness of the data collected.

References

Office for National Statistics (ONS), 2021. Census 2021: Household Questionnaire (England). [online] Available at: https://www.ons.gov.uk/census [Accessed 29 November 2024].

Reflection

Description

Unit 5 of the Research Methods and Professional Practice module focused on interviews, survey methods, and questionnaire design. This unit explored various data collection techniques, highlighting their suitability for qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods research. A significant part of the unit involved understanding the structure, purpose, and limitations of interviews, surveys, and questionnaires, with a particular focus on best practices for effective data collection.

As part of the unit, I engaged in two key activities: the inappropriate use of surveys case study reflective activity and the questionnaires wiki activity. The case study activity required a critical evaluation of survey design flaws and ethical concerns, which helped to develop an awareness of how biased or poorly worded survey questions can distort research outcomes. The wiki activity involved collaboration with peers to discuss best practices for questionnaire design, allowing for an exchange of ideas on how to formulate clear, unbiased, and effective questions.

Another important aspect covered in this unit was the role of questionnaire validation, including techniques such as pre-testing and post-testing to enhance survey accuracy. This unit reinforced the need for careful planning in data collection, as poorly designed surveys can lead to unreliable and invalid results. These insights will be particularly useful in my own research, where designing effective and ethical data collection tools is essential for generating meaningful findings.

Feelings

At the start of this unit, I felt relatively confident in my ability to design surveys and questionnaires, as I had encountered these methods in previous academic and professional settings. However, as I progressed through the unit, I quickly realised that survey and questionnaire design is far more intricate than I had initially thought. The complexities surrounding leading questions, ambiguous wording, response bias, and ethical considerations made me reconsider my previous assumptions.

The inappropriate use of surveys case study reflective activity was particularly eye-opening, as it demonstrated how flawed surveys can lead to misleading conclusions. This realisation was somewhat frustrating, as it highlighted the extent to which surveys are often misused in real-world contexts, particularly in market research and political polling. However, this frustration also motivated me to approach my own research with greater scrutiny, ensuring that my data collection tools are designed in a way that minimises bias and maximises accuracy.

Engaging in the questionnaire's wiki activity was a valuable and collaborative experience, as it allowed me to exchange perspectives with peers and refine my understanding of effective questionnaire design. However, I found it challenging to evaluate the validity and reliability of different questionnaire formats, as I had not previously considered how small changes in wording can significantly impact data accuracy. Despite this challenge, the activity helped me develop a more structured approach to formulating survey questions, reinforcing the importance of clarity, neutrality, and alignment with research objectives.

Evaluation

This unit was particularly beneficial in developing my research skills, especially in questionnaire design, survey methodology, and interview techniques. A major strength I gained from this unit was the ability to critically assess survey design flaws. Through the inappropriate use of surveys case study reflective activity, I learned how leading questions, ambiguous wording, and response bias can distort research findings (Bryman, 2016). This understanding is invaluable, as it will enable me to design robust and reliable data collection tools for my future research projects.

Another positive aspect of this unit was the opportunity to collaborate with peers in the questionnaire's wiki activity. This activity reinforced the importance of peer feedback in refining research tools. Discussing different approaches to questionnaire design highlighted best practices and encouraged me to think more critically about question phrasing and structure. By engaging in these discussions, I gained new perspectives on how to ensure that questions are clear, unbiased, and methodologically sound.

Despite these strengths, I encountered some challenges in understanding and applying questionnaire validation techniques. While I recognised the importance of pre-testing and post-testing, I initially struggled with the practical application of these methods. Ensuring that a questionnaire is both reliable (producing consistent results) and valid (measuring what it intends to measure) is a complex process, and I found myself needing to conduct additional reading to fully grasp the different validation techniques (Creswell & Poth, 2018).

Analysis

This unit reinforced the importance of careful survey and questionnaire design in ensuring research integrity and validity. Before engaging in this unit, I assumed that errors in surveys were primarily the result of technical mistakes rather than methodological flaws. However, I now understand that issues such as question phrasing, response bias, and lack of pre-testing can significantly impact research outcomes (Saunders et al., 2019). This realisation has encouraged me to approach survey design with greater attention to detail, ensuring that each question serves a clear purpose and aligns with research objectives.

The unit also deepened my appreciation for the role of ethics in survey methodology. The inappropriate use of surveys case study reflective activity demonstrated how survey data can be manipulated or misrepresented, leading to misleading or even harmful conclusions. Ethical guidelines such as informed consent, anonymity, and transparency in reporting results must be prioritised to ensure that research adheres to high professional and academic standards (Bell et al., 2022). This insight has encouraged me to be more critical of published survey data, recognising that not all surveys are designed with ethical rigour.

Furthermore, this unit helped me develop a more structured approach to designing questionnaires. I learned that each survey question must serve a specific purpose, rather than being included simply because it seems relevant. This aligns with best practices in research, which emphasise the importance of construct validity—the extent to which a survey accurately measures what it is intended to measure (Kumar, 2019). This knowledge will be invaluable in future research projects, particularly when designing structured and semi-structured interviews.

Conclusion

Unit 5 was an essential learning experience that enhanced my understanding of interviews, surveys, and questionnaire design. It challenged my previous assumptions about data collection, making me realise that minor errors in survey design can have major consequences. The unit helped me develop stronger critical thinking skills in evaluating the reliability, validity, and ethical considerations of survey research.

Although I have made progress in understanding survey methodology, I still need to improve my ability to systematically apply validation techniques. Additionally, I would like to develop a greater awareness of how different survey formats impact response rates and data quality.

Action Plan

To build upon the knowledge and skills developed in Unit 5, I will take a structured approach to further improving my survey and questionnaire design capabilities. Firstly, I will engage in additional reading on questionnaire validation techniques, focusing on key aspects such as construct validity, reliability testing, and pre-testing methods. Understanding these concepts more thoroughly will enable me to design more reliable and valid surveys that accurately measure the intended variables. Books such as Kumar’s Research Methodology: A Step-by-Step Guide for Beginners (2019) and Bell et al.'s Business Research Methods (2022) provide valuable insights into the best practices for ensuring survey accuracy. Additionally, I will review ethical guidelines from professional bodies, such as the British Psychological Society (BPS) and the British Computer Society (BCS), to strengthen my understanding of ethical considerations in survey research and ensure that my surveys adhere to high professional and academic standards.

Secondly, I plan to practise designing and refining surveys, creating small-scale surveys and testing them with peers or colleagues. This will allow me to gain practical experience in identifying potential biases, refining question phrasing, and improving the clarity of response options. Conducting pilot tests of my surveys will also help in assessing whether the questions effectively capture the required data and whether participants interpret them as intended. By receiving constructive feedback from peers, I will be able to adjust and refine my survey structure to improve data reliability.

Furthermore, I will actively seek feedback from lecturers regarding my questionnaire designs. Engaging in discussions with experts will help me to identify potential weaknesses in my approach and gain insights into how to structure questions more effectively. I plan to schedule consultations with my academic mentors and seek opportunities to participate in research groups where survey methodologies are a key focus. Attending webinars on data collection methods will also contribute to my learning, as they provide exposure to new tools and techniques used in professional research settings.

Finally, I will explore the impact of different survey formats on response rates and data quality, as this is an area I found particularly challenging in Unit 5. Understanding how aspects such as survey length, question sequencing, and online vs paper-based formats influence participant engagement will be crucial for improving the effectiveness of my research tools. Reviewing case studies from business research and psychology journals will provide valuable examples of how different questionnaire designs affect data collection outcomes.

By implementing this action plan, I aim to develop a stronger foundation in survey methodology, ensuring that I can design valid, reliable, and ethically sound questionnaires for future research. These improvements will not only benefit my academic work but will also be highly valuable in professional research and industry applications.
