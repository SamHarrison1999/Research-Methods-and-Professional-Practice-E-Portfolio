Unit Overview

In Unit 3, we delved into research methodologies and methods, focusing on research design and the different data collection approaches.

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between exploratory and descriptive research designs.
  • Understand the principles of qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods research.
  • Identify appropriate data collection techniques for various research methods.
  • Evaluate which research methods align best with your chosen research area.

Collaborative Discussion 1 Summary Post

Introduction

After reading through and contributing to the collaborative discussion posts from the other students and receiving feedback from Mario and Thomas on my initial post, I have learnt a lot about the importance of ethics in IT and the role it plays to ensure there has been no wrongdoing. I believe gaining insights from my peers is invaluable as they have a lot more experience than me and were able to provide insights that I had not previously considered.

In my initial post, I explored the ethical, legal and social implications of the malware disruption case study provided by ACM (ACM, 2018). The case study looked at the mistakes made by Rouge Services and how they were complicit with Cyber-criminals using their web hosting services to perform malware attacks. Due to the laws surrounding malware varying from country to country, having a global consensus on the ethical considerations helps to ensure some form of consistency even if there are not legally binding.

Mario’s Response

Mario suggested that I referenced how the ACM Code of Ethics (ACM, 2021) and the BCS Code of Conduct (BCS, 2022) are aligned as there is a lot of overlap between the two. When reading the posts of the other students, I noticed that some of the other students pointed how the specific sections of the ACM Code of Ethics are related to the BCS Code of Conduct. I thought this explanation added a lot of value to their post as it showed how while the ethical guidelines vary between organisations, the general principles are still the same. I agree with Mario that this explanation would have improved the quality of my initial post.

Thomas’s Response

Thomas suggested that I considered the ethical implications of all those involved in the case study not just Rouge services. The reason I didn’t do this in my initial post was due to the suggested word count. Even though I believe my reasoning for not adding this was valid, I agree with Thomas that my post could have benefited from the ethical concerns for those who built the worm that forcibly took Rouge Services offline as it would have demonstrated my ability to look at the bigger picture by considering the legal, ethical and social concerns for everyone involved. For example, while the intentions of the worm were to prevent any further harm, ultimately the worm caused harm to Rouges Services more legitimate clients, thereby breaking ethical principles related to unintentional harm and destruction of data even though they had good intentions. To quote Kristin Hannah, a number one New York Times bestselling author “It’s not intentions that matter, It’s actions”.

Conclusion

While the ethical guidelines provided by ACM (ACM, 2021) and BCS (BCS, 2022) are good idea in theory as they are a useful tool in educating people on what it means to be ethical, the usefulness of them can be brought into question as they are not enforceable as they are merely guidelines and not laws as such individuals and organisations can choose to ignore them.

Seminar Preparation Activity

Peer Review Activity

Introduction

The two sources I will be reviewing in this peer review activity are the articles ‘Artificial Intelligence Techniques and Methodology’ published in AI Magazine (Carbonell & Sleeman, 1982) and ‘Artificial Intelligence Research: A Review on Dominant Themes, Methods, Frameworks, and Future Research Directions’ from The Alliance of Biodiversity International and the International Center for Tropical Agriculture (CIAT) (Ofosu-Ampong, 2024).

Artificial Intelligence Techniques and Methodology

The article in AI Magazine analyses past AI research projects to guide future research. It combines a theoretical analysis with a reflection on the methods AI researchers use to address specific problems and proposes improvements to AI research methodologies through a meta-analysis. As the findings are theoretical, one could question the real-world value.

Artificial Intelligence Research: A Review on Dominant Themes, Methods, Frameworks, and Future Research Directions

The article from CIAT is a comprehensive literature review and thematic analysis of 85 peer reviewed articles on AI research themes and methodologies between 2020 and 2023. The article looks at emerging trends in technology and security issues with AI with the goal of guiding the future of AI research. While the article uses a lot of secondary research, one could argue it doesn’t use enough primary research.

Comparing the studies

The article in AI Magazine focuses on refining the research methodologies used in AI research, whereas the article from CIAT reviews AI research trends and offers insights into emerging areas of focus. The methodologies used in both articles are appropriate (reflective analysis in the article in AI magazine and a systematic review in the article from CIAT).

Both articles rely on data from secondary sources instead of primary research which may cause one to question their validity. Regardless of this, both articles contributed significantly to AI research.

Future Enhancements

Both articles could be enhanced by integrating more empirical data, contemporary trends, and interdisciplinary perspectives to make them more applicable to the current and future directions of AI research. Additionally, they could incorporate primary research and quantitative analyses to provide stronger evidence for the arguments presented, with real-world case studies to have an immediate impact on AI research instead of being mainly theoretical.

Conclusion

While both articles are mainly theoretical and could have benefited from primary research, they certainly do provide value in the field of AI research as they both contributed to the collective body of knowledge.

References

Carbonell, J.G. & Sleeman, D., (1982) Artificial intelligence techniques and methodology. AI Magazine, 3(2), pp.47-56. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1609/aimag.v3i2.371 [Accessed 20 November 2024].

Ofosu-Ampong, K., (2024) Artificial intelligence research: A review on dominant themes, methods, frameworks, and future research directions. CGIAR, 15 November. Available at: https://www.cgiar.org [Accessed 20 November 2024].

Reflection

Description

Unit 3 of the Research Methods and Professional Practice module focused on research evaluation, peer review, and refining research proposals. A significant part of this unit was dedicated to understanding the importance of reviewing existing literature critically and evaluating different research methodologies. Additionally, the unit introduced peer review as a crucial element of academic research, emphasising the role of constructive feedback in refining research quality.

As part of this unit, I engaged in several activities, including the Research Proposal Review Activity, Peer Review Activity, and the Collaborative Discussion Summary Post. These tasks required me to critically assess research proposals, provide structured feedback to peers, and reflect on the key takeaways from collaborative discussions. By participating in these activities, I had the opportunity to apply critical thinking skills and develop a deeper understanding of research design and academic critique.

Additionally, this unit reinforced the significance of academic integrity and ethical considerations in peer review. It emphasised the importance of providing honest, objective, and constructive feedback to ensure that research is rigorous, credible, and ethically sound. This process was particularly insightful, as it allowed me to analyse various research approaches and methodologies, identifying their strengths and weaknesses while also receiving feedback on my own work.

Feelings

At the beginning of Unit 3, I felt somewhat uncertain about my ability to critically evaluate research proposals and provide constructive feedback to peers. While I was familiar with literature reviews, I had limited experience in assessing the quality of research methodologies and identifying gaps in research designs. The idea of reviewing another person's research work seemed challenging because I was unsure whether my feedback would be insightful or helpful enough.

However, as I progressed through the unit, my confidence grew. Engaging in peer review activities and discussions with classmates allowed me to see different perspectives and research approaches. This experience was particularly valuable because it helped me understand the common pitfalls in research proposals, such as unclear research questions, weak justifications for methodologies, and lack of strong supporting evidence from literature.

Receiving feedback on my own research proposal was both constructive and eye-opening. Some of the comments pointed out areas for improvement that I had overlooked, such as refining my research questions and justifying my choice of data collection methods. While I initially felt slightly defensive about some critiques, I soon realised that peer feedback was instrumental in strengthening the quality of my work. This experience highlighted the importance of academic collaboration and critical discourse in refining research ideas.

Evaluation

Overall, Unit 3 was incredibly beneficial in developing my research evaluation and peer review skills. One of my key strengths in this unit was my ability to identify inconsistencies in research proposals and suggest constructive improvements. Through structured peer review exercises, I became more adept at assessing the clarity of research objectives, the feasibility of methodologies, and the reliability of data analysis approaches.

However, one of the challenges I faced was articulating my feedback in a balanced and professional manner. Initially, I found it difficult to provide critical yet constructive comments without sounding overly critical or vague. However, by analysing peer-reviewed articles and instructor feedback, I learned how to frame my critiques in a way that was both supportive and insightful.

Additionally, I found that reviewing other students' proposals helped me recognise gaps in my own research approach. Seeing different ways of structuring literature reviews and research justifications provided me with ideas for improving my own work. This highlighted an important takeaway—peer review is a two-way learning process, where both the reviewer and the reviewee benefit from exchanging feedback.

Analysis

The activities in Unit 3 reinforced the importance of peer review in academic research. In academic publishing, research must go through a rigorous peer review process before being accepted for publication. This ensures that the research is valid, reliable, and contributes meaningfully to existing knowledge (Smith, 2020). By engaging in peer review, I gained first-hand experience of how academic scrutiny and feedback contribute to refining research quality.

This unit also emphasised the role of critical thinking in evaluating research methodologies. I learned that research should not only be assessed for its findings but also for the strength of its design, reliability of data sources, and justification of methodologies (Bryman, 2016). Understanding these aspects helped me improve my ability to identify potential biases, research limitations, and methodological flaws in academic studies.

Furthermore, Unit 3 deepened my understanding of ethical considerations in peer review. Ethical peer review requires honesty, fairness, and objectivity to ensure that research is evaluated based on merit rather than personal bias (Resnik, 2019). I recognised that providing vague or overly harsh criticism could be detrimental to the research process, whereas constructive and well-supported feedback fosters improvement.

Additionally, I realised that evaluating research is an ongoing skill that improves with practice. By actively participating in collaborative discussions and reviewing multiple research proposals, I was able to refine my ability to assess research quality effectively. This will be particularly useful when conducting my own research, as I now have a clearer framework for evaluating literature, designing methodologies, and anticipating potential challenges.

Conclusion

Unit 3 was instrumental in enhancing my ability to critically evaluate research, engage in peer review, and refine my own research proposals. It provided a valuable opportunity to develop key academic skills, including assessing research quality, structuring feedback, and improving research clarity.

One of the most significant takeaways was that peer feedback is an essential part of academic growth. While it may initially seem daunting, receiving constructive criticism allows researchers to refine their work and ensure methodological rigour. Additionally, reviewing others' research helped me identify gaps in my own work, reinforcing the value of collaborative learning in research settings.

Although I improved in analysing research methodologies and providing structured feedback, I recognise that there is always room for growth. Developing stronger argumentation skills and improving the depth of my critiques are areas that I intend to work on further.

Action Plan

To enhance my research evaluation and peer review skills, I will implement the following strategies: firstly, I will engage in regular peer review exercises by participating in academic research forums and peer feedback sessions with classmates. Reviewing research proposals and published papers will allow me to practice evaluating research critically and constructively.

Secondly, I will improve my research methodology analysis by exploring additional academic resources on research design, data collection techniques, and statistical analysis (Bryman, 2016). Attending workshops on qualitative and quantitative research methods will help strengthen my ability to critically assess methodologies.

Additionally, I will refine my argumentation and feedback structuring by studying best practices for providing academic feedback. Ensuring that my critiques are clear, constructive, and well-supported (Resnik, 2019) will be a key focus. Practicing writing structured research critiques will improve my ability to articulate research strengths and weaknesses more effectively.

Finally, I will continue strengthening my ethical awareness in peer review by familiarising myself with academic integrity policies and ethical peer review guidelines from institutions such as the British Computer Society (BCS) and ACM. Ensuring that my feedback remains unbiased, professional, and ethical will be a key focus moving forward.

By implementing these strategies, I aim to further enhance my research evaluation abilities, improve my feedback structuring, and strengthen my critical thinking skills in research review processes.
